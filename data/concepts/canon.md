# The Canon — validated findings of conflict science, as concept cards

Law of this library: cards CLASSIFY situations and inform readings; they NEVER testify about any
actual case. No card contains real-world proper nouns except theorist names. Every card carries an
evidence tag — ROBUST (replicated, broadly accepted) / SUPPORTED (solid but narrower base) /
CONTESTED (live academic dispute) / THEORY (influential, not quantitatively validated) — and a
coding rule naming the ex-ante observables that may instantiate it in a draft, always with cited
evidence. Candidate-term flags mark cards eligible for model-three selection; all others serve
classification and formalizer guidance only.

---

## Family A — Resolve and endurance

**A1. Interest asymmetry (Mack 1975; Arreguín-Toft 2001, 2005).** In asymmetric conflicts the
materially weaker side often prevails because the conflict is existential for it and discretionary
for the stronger side; the weak side's win share has risen over the modern era, and strategy
interaction (direct vs indirect approaches) conditions outcomes. Tag: ROBUST. Coding rule: compare
stated stakes — survival/sovereignty language vs limited-aims language; distance of the theater from
each side's homeland; whether either side frames withdrawal as acceptable. Model term: mostly
REDUNDANT with salience — encode through salience, not as a separate term.

**A2. Casualty sensitivity and support decay (Mueller 1973; Gartner 2008).** Public support for
conflict in broad-accountability polities declines predictably as costs accumulate; early "rally"
spikes fade on a schedule. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: polity accountability breadth; visible
domestic contestation of the conflict; cost exposure of the public. Candidate term: feeds the
comfort/resolve-decay term (with E1).

**A3. Loss-domain risk seeking (Kahneman & Tversky 1979; Levy 1997; McDermott 1998).** Actors who
frame their situation as a loss relative to a reference point gamble; actors in the gain domain play
safe. Predicts escalation by cornered, declining sides exactly where cost-benefit models predict
concession. Tag: ROBUST (psychology) / SUPPORTED (interstate application). Coding rule: does the
actor's own framing describe the status quo as intolerable loss or as holdable gain; recent
trajectory of its position. Candidate term: YES — loss-framing escalation modifier.

**A4. Selectorate logic (Bueno de Mesquita, Smith, Siverson & Morrow 2003).** Leaders answering to
small winning coalitions can privatize benefits and absorb national costs far longer than leaders
answering to broad coalitions, who need policy success; predicts concession timing and pain
tolerance by regime structure. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: size of the leader's essential support
base; recent elite defections; dependence on public goods performance. Candidate term: folds into
cost-tolerance in the belief block.

**A5. Audience costs (Fearon 1994; Schultz 2001; contested by Snyder & Borghard 2011, Trachtenberg
2012).** Leaders facing domestic audiences pay costs for backing down from public threats, which can
make their public commitments more credible. Tag: CONTESTED. Coding rule: publicity level of
commitments; institutionalized domestic accountability; prior public red lines. Use: classification
and credibility reading only — too contested for a model term.

**A6. Democratic war selection (Reiter & Stam 2002).** Broad-accountability polities
disproportionately win the wars they choose, largely by selecting winnable wars; the effect fades in
long wars of attrition. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: who initiated; elapsed duration. Use:
classification only.

**A7. Time horizons and discounting (Streich & Levy 2007).** Actors differ systematically in how
they discount the future; ideological and mission-driven actors credibly play longer games,
accepting present costs for distant payoffs. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: stated planning horizons;
institutional continuity of leadership; track record of waiting out adversaries. Candidate term:
belief-block time-horizon input.

## Family B — Power and its limits

**B1. Nuclear compellence failure (Sechser & Fuhrmann 2017).** Nuclear arsenals deter existential
attack effectively but historically add little to compellence — forcing an adversary to change
behavior; nuclear coercion threats mostly lack credibility for stakes short of survival. Tag:
SUPPORTED. Coding rule: is the demand compellent or deterrent; are stakes existential for the
nuclear actor. Candidate term: YES — capability discount for nuclear weight in compellence
contexts.

**B2. Sanctions base rates (Hufbauer, Schott, Elliott & Oegg 2007; Morgan, Bapat & Kobayashi
2014).** Economic sanctions achieve their stated aims in roughly a quarter to a third of episodes;
success is likelier against friends than adversaries, likelier for modest aims, and less likely
against small-coalition regimes able to shield elites. Tag: ROBUST (as base rates). Coding rule:
aim size; target regime structure; sender-target prior relations. Use: base-rate panel and salience
reading; not a model term.

**B3. Cohesion as force multiplier (military effectiveness literature: Biddle 2004; Castillo 2014;
Talmadge 2015).** Internal unity, morale, and organizational practices multiply or squander nominal
material power; fragmented or coup-proofed forces underperform their capability numbers. Tag:
SUPPORTED. Coding rule: elite fragmentation; desertion/defection indicators; documented
politicization of forces; mobilization capacity. Candidate term: YES — cohesion multiplier on
capability (pairs with E1).

**B4. Occupation resistance (Edelstein 2004).** Attempts to control unwilling foreign populations
face rising nationalist resistance costs over time; success historically requires rare conditions.
Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: whether demands imply foreign control over another population. Use:
classification of feasible outcome ranges.

**B5. Veto players and duration (Tsebelis 2002; Cunningham 2006, 2011).** The more independent
actors whose consent an agreement requires, the longer conflict lasts and the narrower the space of
reachable deals. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: count actors with demonstrated blocking power,
including non-signatory spoilers. Candidate term: possible structural term; partly captured by the
actor table itself.

## Family C — Perception and information

**C1. Rationalist war mechanisms (Fearon 1995).** Absent three frictions — private information with
incentives to misrepresent, commitment problems, and issue indivisibility — bargains dominating war
exist; war occurs through these mechanisms. The foundational classification card: identify which
mechanism drives a given standoff. Tag: ROBUST (theoretical consensus). Coding rule: evidence of
resolve uncertainty; of anticipated power shifts making promises non-credible; of stakes framed as
unsplittable. Use: master classifier; each mechanism routes to different cards.

**C2. War as information revelation (Slantchev 2003; Filson & Werner 2002; Powell 2004).** Fighting
updates beliefs about resolve and strength; settlements become reachable as battlefield information
narrows disagreement — predicting settlement terms that track demonstrated performance. Tag:
SUPPORTED. Coding rule: what the fighting to date has revealed relative to prior expectations. Use:
reading of position ranges mid-conflict.

**C3. Commitment problems and power shifts (Powell 2006).** When power is shifting, the rising side
cannot credibly promise restraint tomorrow, so the declining side may prefer conflict now; predicts
preventive logic and resistance to deals lacking enforcement. Tag: ROBUST (theoretical) / SUPPORTED
(empirical). Coding rule: trajectory of relative capabilities; explicit preventive language. Links
to D1.

**C4. Misperception spirals (Jervis 1976; security dilemma: Herz 1950, Glaser 1997).** Defensive
measures read as offensive intent produce reciprocal escalation without aggressive motives on
either side. Tag: ROBUST (qualitative tradition). Coding rule: documented divergence between one
side's stated intent and the other's characterization of the same act. Links to E2.

**C5. Reputation and past action (Sechser 2010; Weisiger & Yarhi-Milo 2015; skeptics: Press
2005).** Whether backing down previously damages present credibility is disputed; recent behavior
appears to matter under some conditions. Tag: CONTESTED. Coding rule: same-dyad crisis history and
outcomes. Use: classification caution only.

**C6. Intelligence surprise limits (Wohlstetter 1962; Betts 1982).** Surprise succeeds despite
signals because noise, organizational filters, and preconceptions suppress warning; capability
estimates of opaque actors carry systematic uncertainty. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: opacity of an
actor's decision process. Use: widen ranges for opaque actors.

## Family D — Structure of the bargain

**D1. Third-party guarantees (Walter 1997, 2002).** Settlements requiring the weaker side to
disarm or accept vulnerability fail without credible external enforcement; with guarantors they
hold at far higher rates. Among the strongest findings in settlement research. Tag: ROBUST. Coding
rule: does the demanded settlement create post-deal vulnerability; are named guarantors present
with capability and stake. Candidate term: YES — enforcement/guarantee presence.

**D2. Indivisibility and sacred stakes (Toft 2003; Hassner 2003; Goddard 2010).** Stakes framed as
sacred, sovereign, or identity-constitutive resist division and side-payments; disputes over them
settle rarely and late, though reframing can restore divisibility. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule:
sacred/right/sovereignty language attached to the contested issue by an actor's own statements.
Candidate term: YES — indivisibility flag per actor-issue.

**D3. Biased mediators (Kydd 2003; Savun 2008; Beardsley 2011).** Mediators with stakes and
leverage can be more effective than neutrals — their assurances are credible — while mediation
raises short-term agreement odds and can lower long-term durability. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule:
mediator's declared interests and leverage over each side. Use: reading mediator actors' positions
and capability.

**D4. Enduring rivalries (Diehl & Goertz 2000; Colaresi, Rasler & Thompson 2007).** Dyads with
long, punctuated conflict histories behave differently: disputes recur, bargaining carries memory,
and base rates differ from fresh disputes. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: dyadic conflict count and
span. Use: analog retrieval filter.

**D5. Coercive diplomacy success conditions (George 1991; Art & Cronin 2003).** Compellence
succeeds mainly when demands are limited, urgency is real, and — critically — the coercer pairs
threats with credible assurance that compliance ends the punishment. Threats without assurance
fail. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding rule: demand scope; presence of explicit assurances; whether
compliance terms are achievable and verifiable. Candidate term: assurance-presence modifier.

**D6. Golden-bridge/face preservation (Schelling 1960, 1966; O'Neill 1999).** Settlements
requiring public humiliation price in symbolic costs; outcomes that preserve face expand the
reachable space; symbolic concessions can substitute for material ones. Tag: THEORY/SUPPORTED.
Coding rule: publicity of demands; availability of face-saving framings in either side's rhetoric.
Use: reading of feasible settlement shapes.

**D7. Power transition stress (Organski & Kugler 1980; Lemke 2002).** Conflict risk concentrates
where a dissatisfied challenger approaches parity with a dominant power. Tag: SUPPORTED. Coding
rule: capability trajectory ratios plus expressed dissatisfaction with the order. Use:
classification of systemic context.

## Family E — House and historical theories (explicitly lower tier)

**E1. Asabiyyah and comfort decay (Ibn Khaldun, Muqaddimah, 1377).** Group cohesion is the engine
of collective power; prolonged wealth and comfort erode cohesion and the willingness to endure,
degrading effective power independent of material stock. Tag: THEORY — grand-historical, not
quantitatively validated; enters only as the falsifiable hypothesis: the more comfortable side's
effective salience decays with conflict duration; the high-cohesion side's does not. Coding rule:
cohesion observables as in B3 plus duration; comfort proxies (wealth, casualty insulation).
Candidate term: YES — pre-registered decay term, pass/fail on the library.

**E2. Asymmetric perception entry (the Grape Trap — house theory, under peer review).** Stronger
parties accumulate specific distrust that hardens into hostile worldview; weaker parties hold a
hostile worldview that generates specific distrust; leaders' hostile cognition and ground violence
move together with direction unestablished. Tag: THEORY (under review). Coding rule: per-actor —
does hostility appear as itemized grievances or as generalized filter; relative power position.
Candidate term: YES — perception-bias correction, parameters fixed from the source analysis, never
fitted.

**E3. Pattern-lecture corpus (course transcripts).** Applied geopolitical pattern commentary;
classification suggestions only; the standing firewall applies with full force — no claim from this
corpus may ever appear as evidence. Tag: THEORY/UNVALIDATED. Use: template hints only.

---

Selection discipline (standing): the model may carry four to six terms; candidates are chosen by
expected signal and demonstrated non-redundancy against capability × salience; every chosen term is
pre-registered before the library completes; every card, whatever its tag, is outranked by cited
case evidence in any actual draft.
