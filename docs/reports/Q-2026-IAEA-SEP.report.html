Open assumptions — review before solving

  • The 35-member Board composition seated for the September 2026 meeting is identical to the 2025-2026 roster (Argentina, Australia, Belgium, Brazil, Canada, Chile, China, Colombia, Egypt, France, Georgia, Germany, Ghana, India, Italy, Japan, Jordan, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Morocco, Netherlands, Niger, Pakistan, Peru, Philippines, Portugal, Romania, Russia, Saudi Arabia, South Africa, Thailand, Togo, UK, US, Venezuela).
    Sources confirm the 2025-2026 membership and that eleven new members were elected for 2025-2027 at the September 2025 General Conference, but none confirms which roster is seated at the September 2026 Board session, since new terms typically take effect only after the annual General Conference elections held mid-to-late September.
  • Individual members were aggregated into the blocs 'western_aligned' and 'nonaligned_abstainers'.
    Sources jointly treat only the four penholders (US/UK/FR/DE) and the three no-voters (Russia/China/Niger); the remaining yes-voters and abstainers were grouped by the analyst from the 21-3-10 tally, not by any source that defines these as coherent blocs.
  • Niger was aggregated with Russia and China into a single dissenting bloc.
    The only sourced fact is that Niger voted no alongside Russia and China; no source establishes Niger's motive, which likely differs from the two permanent members.
  • Bloc position, salience and capability triangular ranges are inferred from June 10 2026 voting behaviour (yes / abstain / no) as a proxy for September 2026 preferences.
    No source contains explicit September 2026 positions; the vote and the diplomatic-track reporting are the latest available evidence.
  • The capability weighting that places the United States at 100 and downweights the numerically largest (abstaining) bloc reflects a modelling choice about penholder leverage and bloc cohesion.
    The Board's own rules give each member an equal vote; the diplomatic/penholder adjustment is not itself established by any cited source.
  • The Director General/Secretariat is modelled as an outcome-shaping actor.
    Sources show Grossi brokering modalities (Cairo, Oct 2025) and briefing the Board, but do not quantify how much the Secretariat's own preference shifts a Board vote; the salience/capability figures are inferred.
  • Iran is included only as an off-Board influence, not a voting member, with an inferred preferred point near 'close the file'.
    The situation text stipulates Iran is the subject, not a voter; its influence magnitude and preferred point are inferred from its cooperation-suspension law and its June 2026 agreement to re-invite inspectors, not from any statement of a target Board outcome.
  • Salience differentiation (existential for the US, E3, Russia/China, DG and Iran; routine for many non-aligned members) is asserted.
    No source rates per-state issue-salience; it is inferred from each actor's demonstrated engagement with the file.
  • The state of the parallel US-Iran diplomatic track as of the September 2026 meeting is assumed to resemble the June-July 2026 picture (60-day roadmap plus recurring escalation).
    Available reporting stops in July 2026; the September posture is uncertain and no source establishes it.
